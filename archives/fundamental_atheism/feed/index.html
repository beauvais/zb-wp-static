Where I do align myself with a tribe – is where religion seeks to or has acquired special treatment within a society. In the UK membership of a region gives you the right to discriminate against someone's world view and their sexuality; it allows you to discriminate against children in schools and one specific religion gives you a privileged position within the legislature. I happily align myself with a tribe to try to change this.

Does this make me a 'fundamentalist atheist'? You've identified the main conundrum. Some of Ayn Rand's work (e.g., Atlas Shrugged) appealed to me in the past, but I now I believe her world view was flawed (or at least her semantics were flawed). She misled people who remain confused to this day about the value of serving others.

Jesus wasn't divine, but he had a clue. In Bob Dylan's words, you gotta serve somebody. Otherwise, life just isn't fulfilling. The best way to serve is to tap into, develop and deliver on your deepest talents in a way that helps the community at large. The payoff is in improving the lives of others, in making a difference somehow. I'm not religious myself, but I know more religious people who lead by example than I do Randians and other atheists. Hardly a valid sample, I know… But think about how sour and cynical many prominent atheists are. When you write off billions of religious people, you've cut yourself off from the world. That was Rand's fundamental mistake.
